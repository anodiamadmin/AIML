This project centers on electricity demand forecasting, a critical tool for government policy-makers, market bodies, and energy businesses. Accurate forecasting is essential because it directly impacts profitability and cost-efficiency; even slight improvements in prediction can significantly lower costs for consumers and increase margins for providers.
Key Drivers of Demand
Demand is primarily influenced by three factors:
Temperature: The most significant determinant due to heating and air conditioning needs during extreme weather.
Historical Data: Leveraging 20 years of regional trends and macroeconomic variables (e.g., GDP or pandemic impacts).
Past Forecast Accuracy: Analyzing previous models to identify systemic weaknesses.
Core Objectives
The analysis serves three distinct functions:
Forecasting: Moving beyond simple "point estimates" to create sophisticated distributions of future demand.
Synthetic Data Generation: Developing "plausible future worlds" to stress-test planning models. While historical data is deterministic (showing only what did happen), synthetic data provides a probabilistic view of what could happen, overcoming the limitations of short historical records.
Ex-post Analysis: Explaining why specific outcomes occurred to refine future models.
Modeling Approaches
The project acknowledges that different models serve different stakeholders:
Heuristics: For traders needing rapid decisions.
Time Series: For short-term accuracy.
Machine Learning: For complex synthetic data generation and long-term strategic planning.
Ultimately, the "holy grail" is capturing the true distribution of future outcomes, allowing decision-makers to prepare for a wide range of scenarios rather than assuming next year will simply mirror the last.

This report details the development of a high-fidelity dataset for electricity demand forecasting, integrating data from AEMO and Endgame Economics (2010–2021). The methodology transitions from rigorous data hygiene to sophisticated feature engineering and macroeconomic integration.
1. Data Acquisition and Validation
The raw 30-minute interval data underwent a comprehensive validation process to ensure timestamp continuity and record integrity. Irrelevant spatial attributes were removed, and missing temperature or forecast values were addressed via time-based interpolation. The resulting core dataset includes actual demand, temperature, and historical demand forecasts.
2. Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)
EDA confirmed that electricity demand is driven by complex, non-linear relationships:
Thermal Sensitivity: A distinct U-shaped relationship exists between temperature and demand; consumption peaks during extreme heat and cold (HVAC usage) and stabilizes between 18–22°C.
Temporal Cycles: Demand follows rigid intraday patterns (morning/evening peaks) and weekly cycles (higher weekday industrial activity vs. reduced weekend loads).
Forecast Inconsistency: Analysis revealed that existing AEMO forecasts often fail to capture the full magnitude of peaks and troughs, necessitating more robust modeling.
3. Feature Engineering and Transformation
To improve predictive power, the dataset was enriched with several layers of engineered variables:
Temporal Features: Core time variables were transformed using cyclical encoding (sine/cosine) to maintain periodic continuity (e.g., ensuring 23:30 and 00:00 are treated as adjacent).
Calendar Effects: Indicators for public holidays, weekends, and the COVID-19 lockdown period were added to account for behavioral shifts.
Lag and Rolling Statistics: Demand and temperature lags (from 30 minutes to 7 days) and rolling averages were integrated to capture temporal persistence and long-term trends.
Non-linear Interaction Terms: Features like Heating/Cooling Degree Days (HDD/CDD), squared temperature, and a season-sensitive "Peak-Hour" weight were created to model thermal stress more accurately.
4. Macroeconomic and External Drivers
Recognizing that electricity demand is a proxy for economic activity, several long-term indicators were integrated:
Economic Health: Quarterly GDP, annual inflation, and interest rate "shocks."
Market Dynamics: ASX 200 log returns and rolling volatility.
Demographics and Policy: Interpolated population growth (Bankstown), electricity CPI (cost proxy), and the increasing share of renewable/solar energy in the grid.
5. Final Preparation
The final modeling dataset comprises the target variable (TOTALDEMAND) and 35 prioritized features. Correlation analysis confirmed strong linear dependencies for lagged demand, while maintaining lower-correlation features to capture non-linear effects. All numerical data were normalized, and categorical variables were one-hot encoded, resulting in a structured, "model-ready" environment optimized for advanced machine learning algorithms like XGBoost and Neural Networks.
