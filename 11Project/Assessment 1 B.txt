0.2	Brief Literature Review
Electricity demand has increased significantly in recent decades due to population growth, technological development, and expanding urban environments (Nassreddine, 2025). Because electricity is fundamental to industrial production and economic activity, accurate demand forecasting is essential for planning generation capacity, distribution networks, and infrastructure investment (Ahmad, et al., 2018). Reliable forecasts allow energy providers to maintain a balance between electricity supply and demand, whereas inaccurate predictions can create operational and economic challenges. Underestimating demand may lead to power shortages and reduced service reliability, while overestimating demand may result in unnecessary infrastructure expansion and inefficient resource allocation (Kumar Biswajit Debnath, 2018). In Australia, the Australian Energy Market Operator (AEMO) relies on demand forecasting for planning and operational decisions. Even small improvements in forecast accuracy can produce measurable cost savings and reduce exposure to high wholesale prices.
Traditional electricity demand forecasting methods rely on statistical time-series models that analyse historical consumption patterns. Common techniques include the AutoRegressive Integrated Moving Average (ARIMA) model (George E. P. Box, 1970)and exponential smoothing methods (Brown, 1956). These models perform well when electricity demand follows stable temporal patterns such as daily, weekly, and seasonal cycles. For example, García-Ascanio and Maté demonstrated that ARIMA models can produce accurate short-term forecasts for electricity demand when analysing non-stationary time-series data (Carolina García-Ascanio, 2010). However, traditional statistical models generally assume linear relationships and may struggle to capture complex interactions between electricity demand and external variables such as weather conditions, economic activity, and consumer behaviour (Hyndman, 2021). A key driver of electricity demand is weather, particularly temperature. Numerous studies (Behmiri, et al., 2023) show a strong relationship between temperature and load, often described as nonlinear and U-shaped. Demand increases during both high and low temperature conditions due to cooling and heating requirements. In the Australian context, high temperatures are especially important because air conditioning drives peak demand during summer periods. To address these limitations, recent research has explored machine learning approaches for electricity demand forecasting. Artificial Neural Networks (ANN) are widely used because they can model nonlinear relationships between electricity demand and influencing variables (Hippert, et al., 2001). More recently, deep learning models such as Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks have been applied to electricity demand prediction due to their ability to capture temporal dependencies in sequential data (Kong, et al., 2019). Ensemble learning algorithms such as Extreme Gradient Boosting (XGBoost) also demonstrate strong forecasting performance by combining multiple decision trees and efficiently modelling nonlinear relationships within large datasets (Chen & Guestrin, 2016).
Most existing studies rely on a wide range of input variables, including multiple weather factors, calendar effects, and socio-economic data, which can improve accuracy but limit practical use when data is restricted. In addition, many focus on overall performance using metrics such as MAE and RMSE, without recognising that errors during peak demand periods have greater operational and economic impact. In this project, short-term electricity demand forecasting over a 24-hour horizon is enhanced by incorporating the temperature effect. A baseline is established using regression and statistical models, such as ARIMA, and is then compared with machine learning approaches including XGBoost and LSTM. To better reflect real-world conditions, a scenario-based evaluation is introduced, where model performance is assessed during selected extreme temperature periods, both high and low, and across weekdays and weekends. This approach is intended to provide a more realistic assessment of model accuracy and stability during critical demand conditions.

0.3	Methods, Software and Data Description
This project develops and compares models for electricity demand forecasting in New Wales within the National Electricity Market (NEM) (UNSW, 2025). Electricity demand is strongly influenced by temperature, particularly during extreme heat and cold events when heating and cooling needs drive peak consumption. This project investigates short-term electricity demand forecasting in New South Wales using temperature-focused features, with an emphasis on accuracy during critical peak periods. A comparative approach is adopted, evaluating four methods: regression as a baseline, ARIMA to capture time-series patterns, XGBoost for modelling nonlinear relationships, and LSTM for learning temporal dependencies. Historical half-hourly demand and temperature data will be used to train and test the models, with performance assessed using standard metrics such as MAE and RMSE, as well as targeted evaluation during peak demand periods.
A key focus of the project is scenario-based evaluation under extreme temperature conditions. Forecast accuracy will be examined on selected high and low temperature days, with comparisons made between typical weekdays and weekends to capture differences in demand behaviour. Unlike many studies that rely on large numbers of input variables, this project adopts a temperature-driven approach, supported by feature engineering such as lagged demand, time-based variables, and temperature transformations. This allows the study to assess how much predictive power can be achieved with minimal inputs while maintaining reliability under stress conditions. The results aim to identify which modelling approach provides the most accurate and stable forecasts for real-world energy system operations.

The project will use GitHub for version control, Jupyter Notebook for programming and documentation, and both Python and R for analysis. Python libraries such as NumPy and pandas will be used for data manipulation, while Matplotlib and Seaborn will be used for data visualisation. Scikit-learn will support the implementation of machine learning models, and PyTorch will be used for neural network and deep learning approaches. These tools are proposed for the initial development phase and may be refined as the project progresses.
This project uses three CSV datasets: forecastdemand_nsw.csv, totaldemand_nsw.csv, and temperature_nsw.csv (UNSW, 2025), which together support electricity demand forecasting in New South Wales. The totaldemand_nsw dataset provides the actual electricity demand values to be predicted, while temperature_nsw contains air temperature data, which serves as the primary explanatory variable in this study. The forecastdemand_nsw dataset, which is the largest with over 10.9 million records, contains forecast information used for benchmarking and comparison, whereas the temperature and total demand datasets are smaller, with approximately 220 thousand and 196 thousand records, respectively. To enable analysis, the datasets were standardised by aligning time formats, resolving missing and duplicate timestamps, and ensuring consistency across half-hour intervals. The cleaned demand and temperature data were then merged using the datetime attribute to produce a unified dataset suitable for modelling, while the forecast dataset was aggregated to provide a comparable benchmark.

The main features of R Markdown
RMarkdown is a format for writing reproducible, dynamic reports mixing in the same document various languages such as LaTeX, R and Python, among others. It is implemented in the RStudio software. It is similar in spirit to Jupyter notebooks, but it offers more features. In fact, it is possible, directly from within RStudio, to compile (we say "knit") this Rmd file to produce various output formats (such as LaTeX, PDF, Word, Beamer slides, HTML, etc.) thanks to the third-party software pandoc. Using this strategy for creating a report offers both the advantages of a WYSIWYG ("What You See Is What You Get") editor and the flexibility of a typesetting programming language.
When creating an R Markdown document, you intertwine paragraphs of text with chunks of code (e.g., R or Python). You can "play" these chunks by clicking on a green arrow located right of the chunk itself. You thus create your report interactively and iteratively. It is possible to parameterise each chunk individually to either make its content (i.e., the code), or only the output of the code, or nothing, appear in the final document. Moreover, the pieces of text are written in a very simple tag language called Markdown, which makes it easy to read. For instance, to get a text in boldface, write *this text is bold* while for italic, write _this text is in italic_. Of course, you can also use LaTeX commands to typeset mathematics!
Finally, when you are happy with your results, you can knit the whole Rmd document to produce your final PDF or Word report. If a few weeks later, you wonder how you obtained some nice numerical or graphical result displayed in your Word or PDF document, you just need to open the associated Rmd file. You can even change or add a few values in the associated data sets and reproduce the entire updated report in one click!
Please visit the Introduction section at https://rmarkdown.rstudio.com/lesson-1.html to watch a very short video showcasing its use. And, if you are convinced like we are and can find the time, do not hesitate to read the remaining sections. We believe this will greatly improve your data science work efficiency.
Note that you can collaborate online on an RMarkdown document thanks to the trackdown package (see https://bookdown.org/yihui/rmarkdown-cookbook/google-drive.html).
